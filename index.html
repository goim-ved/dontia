Limits

Definition of a Limit

The limit of \(f(x)\) as \(x\) approaches \(a\) is \(L\):

\[\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = L\]

This means that \(f(x)\) can be made as close to \(L\) as desired by taking \(x\) sufficiently close to \(a\).

Limit Laws

For functions \(f\) and \(g\) where \(\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = L\) and \(\lim_{x \to a} g(x) = M\):

  1. \(\lim_{x \to a} [f(x) + g(x)] = L + M\)
  2. \(\lim_{x \to a} [f(x) - g(x)] = L - M\)
  3. \(\lim_{x \to a} [f(x) \cdot g(x)] = L \cdot M\)
  4. \(\lim_{x \to a} \frac{f(x)}{g(x)} = \frac{L}{M}\), where \(M \neq 0\)
  5. \(\lim_{x \to a} [c \cdot f(x)] = c \cdot L\), where \(c\) is a constant

Special Limits

\(\lim_{x \to 0} \frac{\sin x}{x} = 1\)

\(\lim_{x \to 0} \frac{1 - \cos x}{x} = 0\)

\(\lim_{x \to \infty} \left(1 + \frac{1}{x}\right)^x = e\)

Example: Evaluating Limits

Find \(\lim_{x \to 2} \frac{x^2 - 4}{x - 2}\)

Solution:

\(\frac{x^2 - 4}{x - 2} = \frac{(x - 2)(x + 2)}{x - 2} = x + 2\) (for \(x \neq 2\))

Therefore, \(\lim_{x \to 2} \frac{x^2 - 4}{x - 2} = \lim_{x \to 2} (x + 2) = 4\)

Derivatives

Definition of the Derivative

The derivative of a function \(f(x)\) is defined as:

\[f'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(x + h) - f(x)}{h}\]

It represents the rate of change of the function at a point.

Basic Differentiation Rules

  1. Constant Rule: \(\frac{d}{dx}[c] = 0\)
  2. Power Rule: \(\frac{d}{dx}[x^n] = nx^{n-1}\)
  3. Sum Rule: \(\frac{d}{dx}[f(x) + g(x)] = f'(x) + g'(x)\)
  4. Product Rule: \(\frac{d}{dx}[f(x) \cdot g(x)] = f'(x) \cdot g(x) + f(x) \cdot g'(x)\)
  5. Quotient Rule: \(\frac{d}{dx}\left[\frac{f(x)}{g(x)}\right] = \frac{f'(x) \cdot g(x) - f(x) \cdot g'(x)}{[g(x)]^2}\)
  6. Chain Rule: \(\frac{d}{dx}[f(g(x))] = f'(g(x)) \cdot g'(x)\)

Common Derivatives

\(\frac{d}{dx}[\sin x] = \cos x\)

\(\frac{d}{dx}[\cos x] = -\sin x\)

\(\frac{d}{dx}[\tan x] = \sec^2 x\)

\(\frac{d}{dx}[e^x] = e^x\)

\(\frac{d}{dx}[\ln x] = \frac{1}{x}\)

\(\frac{d}{dx}[a^x] = a^x \ln a\)

\(\frac{d}{dx}[\log_a x] = \frac{1}{x \ln a}\)

Applications of Derivatives

Related Rates

If \(x\) and \(y\) are related by an equation, differentiating with respect to time \(t\) gives rates \(\frac{dx}{dt}\) and \(\frac{dy}{dt}\).

Example: For a circle \(x^2 + y^2 = r^2\), differentiating yields:

\[\;2x\frac{dx}{dt} + 2y\frac{dy}{dt} = 0\]

Optimization

To find extrema, set the first derivative to zero:

\[\;f'(x)=0\]

Then use the second derivative test: if \(f''(x) > 0\) it is a local minimum, and if \(f''(x) < 0\) it is a local maximum.

Mean Value Theorem

If \(f(x)\) is continuous on \([a,b]\) and differentiable on \((a,b)\), then there exists \(c\) in \((a,b)\) such that:

\[\;f'(c) = \frac{f(b)-f(a)}{b-a}\]

Integrals

Indefinite Integrals

The antiderivative of \(f(x)\) is given by:

\[\int f(x)\,dx = F(x) + C\]

For example, \(\int x^n\,dx = \frac{x^{n+1}}{n+1} + C\) for \(n \neq -1\).

Definite Integrals

The definite integral from \(a\) to \(b\) is computed as:

\[\int_a^b f(x)\,dx = F(b) - F(a)\]

This is the essence of the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus.

Techniques of Integration

Integration by Parts:

\[\int u\,dv = uv - \int v\,du\]

Substitution Rule:

\[\int f(g(x))g'(x)\,dx = \int f(u)\,du\]

Applications of Integrals

Area Under a Curve

The area under the curve \(f(x)\) from \(a\) to \(b\) is given by:

\[\text{Area} = \int_a^b f(x)\,dx\]

Volume of Revolution (Disk Method)

When revolving \(f(x)\) about the x-axis:

\[V = \pi\int_a^b [f(x)]^2\,dx\]

Volume of Revolution (Shell Method)

For rotation about the y-axis:

\[V = 2\pi\int_a^b x\,f(x)\,dx\]

Arc Length

The length of the curve \(y = f(x)\) from \(a\) to \(b\) is:

\[ L = \int_a^b \sqrt{1 + [f'(x)]^2}\,dx \]

Differential Equations

Separable Differential Equations

A separable differential equation can be written as:

\[\frac{dy}{dx} = g(x)h(y)\]

Separate variables to obtain:

\[\frac{dy}{h(y)} = g(x)\,dx\]

Then integrate both sides.

Linear Differential Equations

A first-order linear equation takes the form:

\[\frac{dy}{dx} + p(x)y = q(x)\]

Using an integrating factor:

\[\mu(x) = e^{\int p(x)\,dx}\]

Series & Sequences

Arithmetic Sequences

The \(n\)th term of an arithmetic sequence is:

\[\displaystyle a_n = a_1 + (n-1)d\]

The sum of the first \(n\) terms is:

\[\displaystyle S_n = \frac{n}{2}\Big(2a_1 + (n-1)d\Big)\]

Geometric Series

The \(n\)th term of a geometric sequence is:

\[\displaystyle a_n = a_1 \cdot r^{n-1}\]

For \(|r| < 1\), the sum to infinity is:

\[\displaystyle S_\infty = \frac{a_1}{1-r}\]

Taylor & Maclaurin Series

A function \(f(x)\) can be expressed as:

\[\displaystyle f(x) = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{f^{(n)}(a)}{n!}(x-a)^n\]

For \(a = 0\), this series is known as the Maclaurin series.

Multivariable Calculus

Partial Derivatives

For a function \(z = f(x, y)\), the partial derivatives are:

\[\frac{\partial z}{\partial x} \quad \text{and} \quad \frac{\partial z}{\partial y}\]

Multiple Integrals

Double integrals over a region \(D\):

\[\iint_D f(x,y)\,dA\]

Triple integrals over a region \(E\):

\[\iiint_E f(x,y,z)\,dV\]

Vector Calculus Theorems

Green's Theorem:

\[\oint_C (L\,dx + M\,dy) = \iint_D \left(\frac{\partial M}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial L}{\partial y}\right)dA\]

Divergence Theorem:

\[\iint_S \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{S} = \iiint_E (\nabla \cdot \mathbf{F})\,dV\]

Stokes' Theorem:

\[\oint_C \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r} = \iint_S (\nabla \times \mathbf{F})\cdot d\mathbf{S}\]
